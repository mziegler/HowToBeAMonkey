About

Nearly every day, researchers at the Lomas Barbudal Monkey Project follow groups of wild capuchin monkeys through a tropical dry forest in Costa Rica to observe their behavior. Since Dr. Susan Perry started studying this population of monkeys in 1990, researchers have been collecting many different types of data about the monkeys, including their foraging and social behaviors, hormone levels (from fecal samples) and genetics. This detailed long-term observation of these wild monkeys helps us understand the evolution of primate intelligence and social behavior.

Winslow Homer is a baby monkey in one of the project's study groups. Some of the researchers followed Winslow Homer all day on January 24, 2014, and you can see some of the data that they collected on this map. You can get a glimpse into the life of a baby capuchin monkey, and learn about some of the data that the Lomas Barbudal Monkey Project collects every day!
